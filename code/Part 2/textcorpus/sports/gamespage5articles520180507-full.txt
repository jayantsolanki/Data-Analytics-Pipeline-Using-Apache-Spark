SPECIAL POST  The Oxford English Dictionary, or OED as it is commonly known, is one of the best known references on the English language, yet a devious clue  It ends with zyzzyva, in brief  stumped many of our solvers in the Thursday, May 3 crossword puzzle by Emily Carroll. It has appeared in The New York Times Crossword a total of 139 times.The fact that the clue stumped people is understandable, since it is out of context compared to an easier clue, like British ref. work. That just goes to show you that you need to be prepared for some topsy-turvy challenges when you solve the Thursday puzzles.The OED was the 1857 brainchild of the Philological Society of London, who decided that existing English language dictionaries were incomplete and deficient. It was a slow and laborious process, as their attempt was to include all English language vocabulary from the Early Middle English period (1150 AD) onward, plus some earlier words if they had continued to be used into Middle English, and the first part (or fascicle) was published in 1884.The complete dictionary  entries are always being added  was finally published in 1928, and the OED, which is published by the Oxford University Press, just celebrated its 90th birthday.Oh, and by the way: A zyzzyva is A genus of tropical weevils (family Curculionidae) native to South America and typically found on or near palm trees.Ways It Might Be CluedBritish ref. work, U.K. lexicon, Voluminous ref, It took 70 years to complete, in brief, It has hundreds of thousands of meanings: Abbr., 21,728-pg. work that is constantly updatedWhat stumped you last week?
